# How Often Should Men Get a Haircut? A Barber's Honest Answer

There's no universal answer here. The right schedule depends on your haircut, your hair type, and how particular you are about how it looks. What we can tell you, after years of watching men walk back in after waiting too long, is that most men wait longer than they should — and they don't always realize it until they're comparing their look now to a photo from three weeks ago.

## The Short Answer

If you want a framework to start from, here it is:

- **Skin fade or bald fade:** every **2–3 weeks**. Fades show regrowth the fastest because any new growth at the sides immediately contrasts against the zero. At four weeks, your fade is functionally gone.
- **Taper or classic cut:** every **3–4 weeks**. The transition is subtler and holds its shape longer. Four weeks is usually the outer edge of still looking intentional.
- **Longer styles, textured cuts, or styles with more length on top:** every **5–6 weeks**. These cuts are more forgiving. Growth adds to the texture rather than destroying the line work.

These are starting points, not rules. Your hair's growth rate might compress these windows. Your tolerance for a slightly grown-out look might stretch them. But if you're asking "how often," this is where to begin.

## Why Most Men Wait Too Long

It's usually one of three things: cost, time, or not noticing the decline.

Cost is understandable, but the math is worth thinking through. A $30 haircut every three weeks is $520 a year. Stretching to every six weeks saves you $260 — but your haircut looks rough for half the time you're wearing it. The value of a cut is front-loaded. It looks best in the first two weeks. After that, you're spending the second half of the cycle looking like you're overdue.

Time is a real constraint. But a barber visit at Clip & Chill — haircut, no waiting — is about 30 minutes. That's less time than most people spend deciding where to eat lunch.

Not noticing the decline is the most common one. Your fade blends out so gradually that you adjust your expectations week by week. You don't notice it's gone until you look at a photo from when it was fresh. A good reference point: ask yourself how the cut looks compared to the day after you got it. If the honest answer is "not remotely the same," you're overdue.

## How Hair Type Affects Your Schedule

Hair type changes the equation significantly — and most men don't account for it.

**Fine, straight hair** shows growth patterns the most clearly. New growth on the sides of a skin fade is visible fast, and the top starts losing its shape earlier than coarser hair would. If your hair is fine, lean toward the shorter end of whatever schedule you're running.

**Thick or coarse hair** grows at the same rate but the density can mask it slightly. That said, thick hair on the sides also expands as it grows — which can make a fade look wide and undefined more quickly than you'd expect.

**Curly or textured hair** doesn't just grow down — it grows outward. A textured crop that was tight and intentional at week two can look like a different cut entirely by week five. Growth on curly hair changes the shape of the cut, not just the length. This is why finding a barber who specializes in textured cuts — like Sido at Clip & Chill — and sticking with them is worth more than the convenience of a random walk-in at any shop nearby.

**Beard growth** runs at roughly half an inch per month for most men. A beard trim every three to four weeks keeps the shape. Every two weeks if you're particular about the neckline and cheek line staying precise and defined.

## Signs You're Due for a Haircut

You probably already know some of these:

- Your fade has blended out and what's left on the sides looks like an unintentional taper.
- Your neckline is undefined — hair is crawling down the back of your neck and it looks unfinished from behind.
- Your part has disappeared into a shapeless mass of overgrown hair.
- You're using more product than usual trying to control a style that no longer has structure underneath it.
- You've started wearing a hat more than usual. Not as a fashion choice.
- You're muting your camera on Zoom calls, and you've only just realized it's not a microphone issue.

None of these are irreversible. But they're all easier to fix the sooner you come in. A cut that's been left four weeks too long takes the barber longer to work with — and the result is rarely as clean as a fresh cut maintained on schedule.

## How to Maximize Value Between Visits

The goal between barber visits is to preserve the shape of the cut, not to recreate it at home.

**Use the right product for your hair type.** A matte clay or paste for structured styles with hold, a light pomade or cream for textured or natural looks. The product should work with the cut's natural movement, not fight it.

**Don't overwash.** Daily shampooing strips natural oils and makes fine hair limp and difficult to style. Two to three times a week is enough for most men. On off days, rinse with water only and let the natural oils do their job.

**Brush or comb with the style's grain.** This sounds obvious but most men don't do it consistently. Thirty seconds every morning brushing your hair in the direction of the style keeps it looking intentional between visits.

**Trim your own neckline carefully — or don't.** If you're confident with a trimmer, a light cleanup of the neckline between visits can extend the life of your cut noticeably. If you're not confident, leave it alone. A botched home neckline is one of the most common things barbers have to fix — and fixing it sometimes means taking more off the back than you wanted.
